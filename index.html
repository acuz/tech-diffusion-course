Part I — Concepts and qualitative models

Quick overview of the topics

We start from the general framework of innovation diffusion of Nelson, Peterhansl & Sampat (2004).

We then study the most common model of the diffusion of a single successful technology, based on Mansfield (1961).

Next we analyse a model of competing technologies introduced by Arthur (1989).

Finally, we look at three recent empirical papers that bring these ideas to data on AI, firm networks and international trade.

Why study technology diffusion?

  • Innovations rarely succeed or fail immediately
  • Adoption takes time, even for clearly superior technologies
  • Understanding diffusion helps us
    • predict technology success / failure
    • design better innovation policies
    • understand why some technologies “lock in” despite alternatives

Key insight: diffusion is not just about the technology itself, but about how it interacts with social systems, networks and existing practices.

The elements of modern diffusion theory

Everett Rogers (1962) identified five key elements:

  1. The innovation itself — what is being adopted?
  2. Communication channels — how information spreads
  3. Time — adoption is a process, not an event
  4. Social system — context matters (networks, norms, opinion leaders)
  5. Adopters — different people adopt at different times

Core question: how, why, and at what rate do new ideas and technologies spread through a population?

The typical diffusion pattern: the S-curve

Slow initial adoption → rapid growth → saturation.

Evolutionary perspective: path dependence

History matters (Dosi, 2023). Three levels of “historicity”:

  1. Weak history — acknowledges time passes
  2. Moderate history — transitions take time and have costs
  3. Strong history — path-dependence: early events can determine long-run outcomes

Mechanisms: dynamic increasing returns (learning by doing, network effects); non-linearities; lock-in to inferior technologies.

Dynamic increasing returns

Supply-side

  • Learning by doing → lower costs
  • Knowledge spillovers → complementary innovations
  • Cumulative discovery

Demand-side

  • Network externalities (more users → more value)
  • Bandwagon effects
  • Coordination / standardisation

Consequence: technologies can dominate not because they are best, but because they got there first or had better early support.

A framework: Nelson et al. (2004)

From theory to framework

We have seen Rogers (characteristics of innovations) and Dosi (path-dependence, increasing returns).

Nelson et al. (2004) ask: can we build a systematic framework that explains why different innovations diffuse in fundamentally different ways?

Two dimensions:

  1. Can we get clear evidence about whether the innovation works?
  2. Do dynamic increasing returns reinforce early adoption?

Differing views on what drives adoption

Adoption of novelty covers many processes — religious practices, dress fashions, medical treatments, farming practices, business methods.

  • Economists: profitability
  • Historians: objective technological criteria shared by potential adopters
  • Sociologists: socially constructed criteria, part of the process of acceptance or rejection

Nelson et al. (2004)’s two dimensions

  • Persuasive feedback — the extent to which it is possible to generate clear, widely persuasive evidence on whether an innovation works (experiments; sharp feedback in use).
  • Dynamic increasing returns — widespread adoption enhances the value of adoption for those who have not adopted yet: network effects, complementary activities, norms and expectations.

Hybrid corn (1920s–1940s)

📱 Vote in the pool — which of the four models?

Hybrid corn — discussion

Model I (rational choice), with elements of Model III.

  • Late 1920s: a radical innovation for feed; hybrid seed gave markedly higher yields
  • Rapid adoption driven by clear, measurable profitability — the textbook rational case
  • Channels: government experiment stations, farm bulletins, seed-certification programmes, “corn contests”, and neighbouring farmers (local imitation)
  • Griliches (1957): differences in diffusion speed across US states tracked expected profitability (market size, seed-firm entry)
  • Pulls toward Model III: needs complementary practices; dynamic increasing returns (hybrids reinforced further R&D); hybrids don’t breed true → farmers must buy seed every year
  • Berlan & Lewontin / Kloppenburg: partly a social construction by seed firms, who favoured hybrids over open-pollinated improvement

Ryan & Gross (1943); Griliches (1957); Berlan & Lewontin (1986); Kloppenburg (1988).

Aircraft retractable landing gear (1930s–1940s)

📱 Vote in the pool — which of the four models?

Landing gear — discussion

Model I (rational-choice diffusion) — strong, quantitative feedback.

  • Pre-1930 essentially all aircraft had fixed gear; by ~1949 all high-performance aircraft had retractable gear
  • Trigger: more powerful engines → speeds >200 mph, where drag dominates the weight/complexity penalty
  • Competing criteria: low drag (retractable) vs lower weight, reliability, simpler maintenance (fixed, early on)
  • Intermediate step: streamlined “spatted”/trousered fixed gear bought time while retraction mechanisms matured
  • As speeds rose and reliability improved, drag savings decisively won → near-universal adoption
  • Performance was measurable → little ambiguity, no lock-in

Vincenti (1994).

Polio vaccines (1950s–1960s)

📱 Vote in the pool — which of the four models?

Polio vaccines — discussion

Model I (rational choice) with rapid technology switching.

  • 1949: three poliovirus serotypes identified → vaccine development becomes feasible
  • Two designs competed: Salk (killed, injected; faster to field, immunity duration uncertain) vs Sabin (live attenuated, oral; slower to develop, longer/passive immunity)
  • 1954: a huge field trial (~1.8M “Polio Pioneers”); 1955 results → dramatic case reduction → immediate licensing
  • Strong, public persuasive feedback (visible disease, clear endpoints) → fast, rational adoption
  • 1961–63: switch Salk → Sabin once oral advantages were clear → low switching costs, no lock-in
  • Impact: ~90% reduction in incidence by 1961

Robbins (1999); Salk et al. (1994); Blume & Geesink (2000).

Screening mammography (1960s–present)

📱 Vote in the pool — which of the four models?

Screening mammography — discussion

Model III (social construction) — despite seemingly objective criteria.

  • 1930s: X-rays for diagnosis; 1960s: screening of asymptomatic women on the early-detection theory
  • 1963 HIP study (~62,000 women) suggested a mortality reduction — but evidence stayed contested for decades
  • Genuine ambiguity: over-diagnosis / over-treatment of non-lethal cancers, radiation exposure, lead-time and length biases; sharpest disagreement for women under 50
  • Social-construction elements: military metaphors (“war on cancer”), gender and power relations, cultural assumptions about women’s responsibility for health
  • Politics over evidence: 1997 US Senate votes 98–0 against a cautious consensus panel; 1999 Swedish re-analysis finds no clear benefit
  • Same data, persistently different conclusions → weak persuasive feedback

Lerner (1998, 2001); Fletcher (1997); Olsen & Gotzsche (2001).

M-form organisation (1920s–1970s)

📱 Vote in the pool — which of the four models?

M-form — discussion

Model I (rational choice) with strong elements of Model III.

  • Problem: large diversified firms overwhelmed central management → solution = decentralise into semi-autonomous product/market divisions; HQ does strategy + capital allocation
  • Adoption among the largest US firms: ~2% (1919) → ~84% (1979)
  • Rational case: a coherent answer to a real coordination problem; promoted by McKinsey, management journals, business schools
  • Social-construction case: a “fuzzy concept”, hard to define; many “mixed/bastardised” versions adopted
  • Hard to isolate M-form’s effect on profitability → weak persuasive feedback
  • Mimetic adoption: pressure to conform to what leading firms and consultants endorse

Chandler (1962); Williamson (1975); Fligstein (1985); DiMaggio & Powell (1983).

QWERTY vs DVORAK keyboards

📱 Vote in the pool — which of the four models?

QWERTY vs DVORAK — discussion

Model II (quasi-rational, lock-in) — the canonical path-dependence case.

  • QWERTY (1870s) arranged keys to reduce typebar jams on mechanical typewriters — a constraint long obsolete
  • DVORAK (1930s) designed for typing speed/efficiency; yet QWERTY still dominates
  • Lock-in mechanisms: network effects (shared standard), switching costs (retraining typists), complementary assets (typing schools, textbooks), self-reinforcement (more users → more support → more users)
  • David (1985): a classic example of inefficient lock-in — history, not merit, selected the standard
  • Liebowitz & Margolis (1990), “The Fable of the Keys”: dispute DVORAK’s measured superiority → how inefficient the lock-in really is remains debated

David (1985), AER; Liebowitz & Margolis (1990), JLE.

Quality circles (1980s)

📱 Vote in the pool — which of the four models?

Quality circles — discussion

Model IV (fad / fashion), with Model III features.

  • Early-mid 1980s: Japanese firms outcompete US firms → US firms import Japanese practices — small worker groups meeting to improve quality
  • A loose umbrella of practices labelled “quality circles”
  • Social construction: a surge of articles, vivid case stories, but unconvincing statistics; heavy advocacy by researchers and consultants; hard to define or measure effects
  • Fad signature: fragile expert consensus and no strong increasing returns to adoption
  • Sharp decline in articles — and in firms keeping the structures — after the mid-1980s
  • Missing the two anchors of durable diffusion: sharp performance evidence and increasing returns

Meyer & Rowan (1977); Abrahamson (1996); Abrahamson & Fairchild (1997).

AC vs DC electrical systems

📱 Vote in the pool — which of the four models?

AC vs DC — discussion

Model II (quasi-rational, lock-in).

  • 1880s “War of Currents”: Edison (DC) vs Westinghouse/Tesla (AC)
  • Trade-offs: DC safer at low voltage, simpler, battery-friendly; AC allows voltage transformation → efficient long-distance transmission
  • Tipping event: AC chosen for the Niagara Falls plant (1895) → momentum
  • Lock-in: standardisation of equipment/infrastructure; increasing returns (more AC → more AC kit → lower costs) → AC becomes the universal grid standard
  • Modern irony: digital devices and much renewable generation are DC; HVDC now carries very-long-distance links — the “loser” returns where its physics wins

David (1985); Hughes (1983), “Networks of Power”; McNichol (2006).

Part II — Formal models and modern evidence

Mansfield (1961): a successful innovation

“Once an innovation is introduced by one firm, how soon do others in the industry come to use it? What factors determine how rapidly they follow?” — Mansfield (1961)

  • Data on 12 innovations in four industrial sectors
  • A deterministic model of progressive adoption

What the data show (1/3)

Innovations: by-product coke oven, diesel locomotive, tin container, shuttle car (iron & steel, railroads, brewing, coal).

What the data show (2/3)

Innovations: car retarder, trackless mobile loader, continuous mining machine, pallet-loading machine.

What the data show (3/3)

Innovations: continuous wide-strip mill, centralized traffic control, continuous annealing, high-speed bottle filler.

The logistic model: the core idea

The rate of adoption depends on two factors:

  1. Natural rate — how profitable / attractive is the innovation?
  2. Imitation effect — how does seeing others adopt influence your decision?

Key insight: the more firms have already adopted, the faster the rest adopt (information spreads, uncertainty falls) — generating the S-curve.

Structure of the model

Time is continuous; \(n\) potential adopters, \(n(t)\le n\) already adopted.

Adopters in a small interval \(\delta\): \[ n(t+\delta)-n(t)=\lambda(t)\,(n-n(t))\,\delta. \]

With \(x(t)=n(t)/n\) and \(\delta\to 0\): \[ \dot{x}(t)=\lambda(t)\,(1-x(t)). \]

The adoption rate

\[ \lambda(t)=a_1+a_2 x+a_3\pi+a_4 c+a_5 x\pi+a_6 x c+\dots \]

Rewrite \(\lambda(t)=\lambda_0+\lambda_1 x(t)\):

  • \(\lambda_0=a_1+a_3\pi+a_4 c\) — natural rate of adoption
  • \(\lambda_1=a_2+a_5\pi+a_6 c\) — effect of previous adopters (\(\lambda_1>0\Rightarrow\) dynamic increasing returns)

Solving the model

\[ \dot{x}(t)=(\lambda_0+\lambda_1 x)(1-x). \]

With \(x(t_0)=x_0\): \[ x(t)=\frac{(\lambda_0+\lambda_1 x_0)e^{(\lambda_0+\lambda_1)(t-t_0)}-\lambda_0(1-x_0)} {(\lambda_0+\lambda_1 x_0)e^{(\lambda_0+\lambda_1)(t-t_0)}+\lambda_1(1-x_0)}. \]

For \(t_0=0,\;x_0=0\;(\lambda_0\neq 0)\): \[ x(t)=\frac{e^{(\lambda_0+\lambda_1)t}-1}{e^{(\lambda_0+\lambda_1)t}+\lambda_1/\lambda_0}. \]

Observations

  • The logistic case is \(\lambda_0=0\) (needs \(x_0>0\)).
  • If \(\lambda_0+\lambda_1>0\): \(x\to 1\). If \(\lambda_0+\lambda_1<0\): \(x\to -\lambda_0/\lambda_1>0\).
  • Mansfield’s estimates: \(\lambda_1\in(0.2,0.5)\), with one case \(\lambda_1=2.4\).

Mansfield’s findings

Faster diffusion when: higher profitability (stronger advantage); lower costs; stronger imitation effects.

The imitation parameter ranged from 0.2 to 2.4 — higher values mean faster acceleration once adoption begins.

Key limitation: the model assumes everyone eventually adopts — it does not explain failed diffusion or competing technologies.

Arthur (1989): when technologies compete

Two technologies; the return to a technology rises with its own adoption: \[ \pi_A=a_A+r_A n_A,\qquad \pi_B=a_B+r_B n_B. \]

Questions: will one dominate or both coexist? Does the better technology always win? How do early choices matter?

Insight: the answer depends on increasing, decreasing or constant returns to adoption.

Structure of the model

Time is discrete; two technologies \(i=1,2\). At each date a new agent picks the technology with the higher payoff \(\pi_i(t)\), which can depend on the number of adopters.

Population share \(x_i(t)=n_i(t)/t\) (a share over choices made so far, unlike Mansfield’s \(x=n(t)/n\)).

The process can be probabilistic; we study its dynamics under increasing, decreasing and constant returns.

Properties of the adoption process

  • Predictable — the observer can state ex-ante a path \(\hat{x}_i(t)\) with \(|x_i-\hat{x}_i|\to 0\) a.s.
  • Ergodic — the stochastic detail does not matter: two realisations converge.
  • Flexible — the subsidy needed to change long-run adoption is bounded.
  • Efficient — if \(\pi_i>\pi_j\) at \((z_i,z_j)\) with \(z_i>z_j\), then \(\pi_i>\pi_j\) also at \((z_i,z)\) for all \(z\le z_i\).

Homogeneous adopters

Payoff \(\pi_i=a_i+r_i\,n_i(t-1)\).

  • Decreasing returns (\(r_1,r_2<0\)): shares settle; predictable, ergodic, efficient and flexible — the “well-behaved” case.
  • Increasing returns (\(r_1,r_2>0\), learning by using): generally one technology dominates; predictable and ergodic but inflexible and possibly inefficient — a possible lock-in.

Homogeneous adopters: the two regimes

Decreasing returns — shares settle.

Increasing returns — one technology takes over.

Heterogeneous adopters

Two agent types arrive at random; payoff \(\pi_i=a^j_i+r\,n_i(t-1)\), with \(a^1_1>a^1_2\), \(a^2_1<a^2_2\).

Define \(d(t)=n_1(t)-n_2(t)\):

  • \(r=0\) — random walk (no barriers)
  • \(r<0\) — reflecting barriers at \(d=(a^i_1-a^i_2)/(-r)\) → stable coexistence
  • \(r>0\) — absorbing barriers at \(d=(a^i_1-a^i_2)/r\) → lock-in

Heterogeneous adopters: the three regimes

\(r=0\): random walk

\(r<0\): reflecting

\(r>0\): absorbing (lock-in)

Properties of the three regimes

Predictable Flexible Ergodic Efficient
Constant returns Yes No Yes Yes
Decreasing returns Yes Yes Yes Yes
Increasing returns No No No No

With increasing returns, history matters: early random events can select the winner — possibly an inferior technology (lock-in).

Interactive: competing technologies

Set \(a^j_i\), the return \(r\), the seed and horizon → watch \(d(t)\) and the barriers. Arthur tab: under \(r>0\), change only the seed and the winner changes (non-ergodicity); \(r<0\) gives stable coexistence.

Connecting the models to Nelson’s framework

  • Mansfield’s logistic model ≈ Model I (rational choice): clear feedback, single technology, focus on the rate of adoption.
  • Arthur’s competing-technologies model ≈ Model II (quasi-rational, lock-in): increasing returns, focus on which technology wins.

Different mechanisms (profitability vs network effects vs social construction) → fundamentally different diffusion patterns.

Modern empirics: three studies

From historical cases to modern digital GPTs

Nelson et al. (2004) studied 20th-century innovations. What about 21st-century general-purpose digital technologies — AI, machine learning, big data, IoT, blockchain?

How they differ from the historical cases:

  • Intangibility — digital and embedded in services; harder to observe and measure than a machine or a vaccine
  • Speed — potentially much faster diffusion, but also faster lock-in
  • Network effects — value rises steeply with adoption (stronger than past GPTs)
  • Global — knowledge is far more mobile across borders
  • Uncertainty — productivity benefits are unclear and hard to measure (the “productivity paradox”)

Research challenge: with no clean adoption register, how do we measure who actually uses an emerging technology, and identify how it spreads?

Measuring modern diffusion: the data challenge

Indicator Strengths Limitations
Patents Objective, detailed Lag actual use; miss adoption
R&D spending Input measure Not available at fine granularity
Surveys Direct questions Small samples; response bias

New approaches: web scraping, NLP/LLMs, network data, digital traces — real-time, comprehensive, but with measurement error and selection.

Dahlke et al. (2024) — AI diffusion in firm networks

Sample: 1.1M firms in Germany, Austria, Switzerland (2022). Method: web scraping + transformer LM → deep vs superficial AI knowledge (categories overlap).

  • Overall AI adoption 2.2% (1.5% deep, 1.3% superficial)
  • Highly geographically (Munich, Hamburg, research hubs) and sectorally concentrated (ICT >11%, consulting >5%)
  • AI adopters form closed, interconnected clusters, peripheral to the broader network (~4% of firms linked to an AI-positive firm)

Geographic concentration (Dahlke)

Hot-spots around major cities and German AI research centres; many regions near zero; hot-spots show a higher share of deep vs superficial knowledge.

Network structure (Dahlke)

AI adopters are highly interconnected among themselves but peripheral to the overall firm network — a closed system that hinders broader diffusion.

Three epidemic mechanisms (Dahlke)

Three channels through which AI adoption spreads (average marginal effects on having deep AI knowledge):

  1. Mimetic pressure (indirect exposure) — being surrounded by adopters: regional adoption rate AME 0.192***, sectoral rate 0.200*** → strong pull inside hot-spots.
  2. Direct transmission (firm-to-firm links) — reciprocal deep-AI hyperlinks AME 0.201***; superficial-knowledge links are weak; moderated by cognitive (not geographic) proximity.
  3. Network embeddedness (structural position) — degree centrality in the AI network AME 0.259*** (largest effect); closeness in the overall firm network ≈ 0 / negative; betweenness small.

Reading: AI knowledge circulates inside a closed club of well-connected adopters that sits peripheral to the broader firm network → the classic epidemic (susceptible firms catch it from neighbours) breaks down.

Ayerst et al. (2023) — embodied technology in trade

Core idea: importing a product can mean importing the knowledge embedded in it — an imported MRI machine carries image-processing algorithms, materials science and software design that local firms can learn from, reverse-engineer and build on.

Two distinct kinds of sectoral linkage:

  • Production linkages — which sectors supply physical inputs to yours (from input-output tables). E.g. autos buy from steel.
  • Knowledge linkages — which sectors supply knowledge inputs for your innovation (from patent citations across sectors). E.g. pharma cites biotech and software.

Key finding: the two are empirically distinct — a sector buys goods from one set of sectors but cites patents from a different set. So knowledge does not flow along the same paths as goods.

Knowledge vs production linkages (Ayerst)

Different inter-sectoral patterns; knowledge linkages less concentrated; more persistent for the US (less so in other countries).

Constructing embodied-technology measures

\[ \text{EmbTechK}^h_{i,t}=\prod_{l\neq h}\Big(1+\textstyle\sum_{p\in P_l}w^l_{(p)}K^p_{\text{US},t}M^p_{\text{US},i,t}\Big)^{\kappa^{l,h}_{\text{US},t}} \] \[ \text{EmbTechP}^h_{i,t}=\prod_{l\neq h}\Big(1+\textstyle\sum_{p\in P_l}w^l_{(p)}K^p_{\text{US},t}M^p_{\text{US},i,t}\Big)^{\rho^{l,h}_{\text{US},t}} \]

\(K\): US subsector knowledge stock; \(M\): imports from the US; \(\kappa\): knowledge IO weights; \(\rho\): production IO weights. Own sector \(l\neq h\) excluded.

Do imports drive innovation?

82 countries, 292 sectors, 1995–2015; IV = US exports to dissimilar countries (outside the importer’s similarity cluster).

Outcome Knowledge-weighted Production-weighted
Elasticity (patents) 0.666*** 0.003**
Elasticity (fwd. cites) 0.545*** 0.002*
US backward citations 1.023*** 0.005*

A 1% rise in knowledge-weighted imports → ≈0.67% more patenting; knowledge linkages are orders of magnitude more effective than production linkages.

Sun & Trefler (2023) — AI, data regulation & trade

Setting: mobile apps — a fast-growing, AI-intensive, heavily-traded digital service. 35,575 apps (2015–2020), users by country.

Measurement: link app descriptions to developers’ AI patents with Google’s BERT (cosine similarity) — billions of app–patent pairs.

Identification: a Heckscher-Ohlin cost-shifter instrument (industry AI-intensity × country AI-abundance × pre-2008 AI patents), \(F\approx 1{,}384\).

Sun & Trefler — key results

  • AI raises foreign users by 2.67 log points (a >10-fold increase); robust, with placebos centred on zero.
  • Cross-border data restrictions roughly halve the effect (2.58 → 2.02 log points).
  • Autocracies impose the tightest rules and suffer most — e.g. China ≈ 0.68 log pts (≈50% fewer foreign users) vs ≈ 0 for the US/Canada.
  • AI externality: firms with no patents of their own still gain from others’ AI corpus (≈0.3 log points) — spillovers reach almost all exporters.

The “click-throughs −75% / views −33% / purchases −81%” figures are a separate cited field experiment (Alibaba), not Sun & Trefler’s own autocracy estimate.

Synthesis: domestic and international channels

Mechanism Across firms (Dahlke) Across countries (Ayerst)
Indirect Regional/sectoral mimetic pressure Trade exposure to frontier knowledge
Direct Firm hyperlinks, reciprocal links Knowledge-weighted imports
Network position Embeddedness in AI network Position in global value chains
Moderators Cognitive proximity Knowledge vs production linkages

Plus regulation (Sun & Trefler): cross-border data rules shape where AI services can diffuse.

Channels of technology diffusion

Across firms (Dahlke): AI knowledge trapped in closed clusters; peripheral firms excluded even when geographically close; mimetic pressure strong in hot-spots; embeddedness crucial but creates closure.

Across countries (Ayerst): need absorptive capacity; knowledge linkages > production linkages; importing the “wrong” products misses spillovers; own knowledge stock complements imported knowledge.

Regulation (Sun & Trefler): cross-border data rules shape where AI-enabled services can diffuse.

Methodological lessons

  1. Measurement innovation is crucial — web scraping, NLP/LLMs, network data complement patents, R&D and surveys.
  2. Multiple data sources increase confidence — triangulation addresses measurement error and selection.
  3. Granularity vs coverage trade-off — firm-level (Dahlke, DACH) vs country-sector (Ayerst, 82 countries).
